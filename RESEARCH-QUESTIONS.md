# Open research questions

This document treats the ten open issues from §15 of the design spec at [`cardano-wiki/docs/superpowers/specs/2026-05-03-omega-archive-anchored-claims-design.md`](cardano-wiki/docs/superpowers/specs/2026-05-03-omega-archive-anchored-claims-design.md) at depth. Each issue gets five paragraphs covering what the question is, why it remains open, the decision space, what the rest of the design depends on it for, and the concrete path to closure. The spec itself is the canonical reference; this document is the long-form companion that pulls each open question out of its one-line bullet and gives it the room to breathe.

The questions split across four shapes. Some are research-paper-shaped: they need a published cryptographic construction with a security reduction before any implementation work makes sense. Others are governance-shaped: a binary or a tunable that the protocol owner cannot pick alone, and which CIP-1694 governance is the only legitimate decision-maker for. A third group is engineering-shaped: the path is clear, the answer is conditional on scope and effort, and the question is which sub-deliverable lands first. The last group is upstream-tracking-shaped: Omega depends on a deliverable that another open-source project owns, and the right action is to track without forking. The wrap-up section maps each question to its shape.

## 1. Hash-based VRF construction

The §1 cryptographic primitives table requires a verifiable random function with a Praos-equivalent uniqueness reduction, but specifies the construction as TBD with a footnote that any candidate must not reduce to "no collisions in H." The Ouroboros Praos paper (Kerber-Kiayias-Russell-Russell, eprint 2017/573) proves adaptive security against an actively malicious adversary using a curve-based VRF whose uniqueness comes from the hardness of computing discrete logs in a prime-order group. The Omega program forbids curve operations everywhere per §1, so the VRF has to come from somewhere else. The leading hash-based candidate, X-VRF by Buser, Dowsley, and Esgin, was broken at Financial Crypto 2024 by Bodaghi and Safavi-Naini in [a uniqueness attack](https://www.ifca.ai/fc24/preproceedings/213.pdf). "Closed" looks like a published construction with a uniqueness theorem under standard cryptographic assumptions plus a Plonky3-friendly verifier circuit, plus a reduction theorem showing Crypsinous's adaptive-security proof transfers to the new VRF without re-proving the entire chain.

The reason this is still open is that the original Praos uniqueness proof leans on DDH-style assumptions in the curve group, and replacing the curve VRF means re-proving a security claim that was load-bearing for the entire Ouroboros family. The general-purpose hash-based VRF literature (Goldberg-Naor-Reyzin from 1999 onward) was not designed for adaptive-security blockchain consensus. It was designed for set-membership applications where uniqueness against passive adversaries is enough. X-VRF was the first serious attempt to bridge that gap; the FC 2024 break shows the bridge is harder than the authors thought. Other candidates exist in the literature, but none have a published Praos-equivalent reduction. The research community has not yet produced a hash-based VRF that simultaneously satisfies post-quantum hardness, in-circuit efficiency for STARK verifiers, and adaptive uniqueness under malicious key generation.

The decision space has three concrete options. Option (a) is leanXMSS plus a SNARK wrapper: build a one-time-signature-based VRF where the SNARK proves correct evaluation, with a custom uniqueness proof over the underlying hash. The cost is a custom security analysis that probably needs academic review; the benefit is that the construction is mostly composed from existing primitives. Option (b) is a Poseidon2-based VRF inside a STARK with a published security analysis. Plonky3-native, in-circuit cheap, but requires a paper showing Poseidon2's cryptographic strength is adequate for VRF uniqueness, not just hash-collision resistance. Option (c) is the Algorand-style fallback: skip a true PQ VRF in v1 and use a randomness beacon constructed from FN-DSA signatures plus a verifiable delay function. Algorand has shipped this pattern in production. The cost is that the consensus protocol becomes a hybrid (signature-beacon plus VDF instead of a pure VRF), which complicates the Crypsinous adaptive-security reduction in a different way.

Without this question closed, the entire §7 consensus stack has no clean PQ implementation path. PQ-Crypsinous, PQ-Chronos, and PQ-Minotaur all consume the same VRF; closing the VRF question closes the cryptographic core of all three at once. This is genesis-blocking: the protocol cannot ship a testnet with a known-broken VRF, and waiting until v∞ is not viable because consensus is the layer that has to work from block 1. The other tracks (T6 verifier circuit, T7 bridge protocol) are insulated from the VRF choice and can proceed in parallel, but T2 consensus blocks on this and T11 testnet operations blocks on T2.

Resolution falls into two paths: pin a published paper or commission one. Pinning means waiting for the academic community to ship a construction, then adopting it. Commissioning means a paid academic engagement with a specific deliverable. The Filecoin precedent is informative here: Filecoin commissioned multiple security papers from academic groups during its construction, and those papers were published independently. Estimated time to publication-grade construction is six to twelve months, comparable to similar cryptographic deliverables that have been commissioned in the past. The decision-maker is the protocol-design lead in coordination with the research community; the Cardano Foundation or IOG could fund the commissioning if it goes that route. The deliverable is a paper plus reference implementation plus Plonky3 circuit, not a paper alone.

## 2. Lattice-vs-hash signature decision

Per §1, three NIST-standardised PQ signature schemes are candidates for Omega's user-signing layer: ML-DSA-65 (FIPS 204, ~3.3 KB signatures), FN-DSA-512 (FIPS 206 IPD, ~666 B signatures, also known as Falcon), and SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s (FIPS 205, 7.8 KB to 50 KB depending on parameter set). The genesis-ceremony attestations and the governance / KES root are already locked to SLH-DSA per §1 and §3 because hash-based schemes have no algebraic structure for an attacker to attack: their security reduces directly to the underlying hash function's collision and second-preimage resistance. The open question is which scheme does ordinary on-chain user-signed transactions use. Closed looks like a definitive choice, a parameter set, and a reference benchmark on representative hardware showing transactions per second under realistic chain load.

The question is open because the trade-offs are genuinely close, not because the literature is missing. Cloudflare's [2025 analysis of post-quantum TLS performance](https://blog.cloudflare.com/pq-2025/) argued that lattice schemes are operationally viable for high-throughput applications and that the signature size cost is acceptable for transactions but not for connection handshakes. ML-DSA-65 has been extensively reviewed by NIST and is now FIPS 204 Final. FN-DSA-512 is faster to verify and produces smaller signatures, but is harder to implement in constant time and has Gaussian-sampling subtleties that have caused implementation bugs in earlier Falcon versions. SLH-DSA is the conservative choice with the cleanest security argument, but its signatures are 10x to 50x larger than the lattice options, which has direct effects on block size, mempool capacity, and bandwidth.

The decision space is the three options above plus their parameter variants. ML-DSA-65 gets you 3.3 KB signatures, fast signing and verification, NIST-standardised, and acceptably reviewed. The cost is dependence on a structured-lattice hardness assumption (Module-LWE) that is newer than hash-function security. FN-DSA-512 gets you the smallest signatures of the three (~666 B), making it close to Ed25519 in on-chain footprint, but at the cost of the trickier implementation and the fact that FIPS 206 is still IPD as of late 2025. SLH-DSA-only gets you the cleanest no-curves-anywhere posture and the strongest cryptographic argument, but at the cost of bloating every transaction to several KB minimum. Algorand has shipped FN-DSA in production at the consensus layer; Bitcoin's BIP-360 proposal favours ML-DSA. No major chain has committed to SLH-DSA-only for ordinary user transactions.

This question gates §10 PQ-key derivation (the BIP-39 → BIP-32 → BIP-85 → signature derivation rule has to fix the target signature scheme), §9 wallet requirements (hardware wallets need to compute the chosen signature on-device), and §1 generally because the leaf-format domain tags depend on which signature is over which payload. It does not gate genesis: the genesis ceremony attestations are SLH-DSA regardless of what user-signing turns out to be. It is v1.0-blocking on the user-side wallet implementations, which means it has to be settled before T8 wallet work starts, and it interacts with T7 bridge protocol because claim transactions carry user signatures.

Resolution is a protocol-design-lead decision informed by benchmarks plus a reference test against representative wallet hardware. The realistic outcome is a primary scheme plus a fallback: ML-DSA-65 for ordinary transactions because the lattice option dominates on the size-versus-throughput Pareto frontier, with SLH-DSA accepted at the verifier as the "always-valid" backup for users who refuse to accept any lattice assumption. FN-DSA-512 stays optional and gets adopted later if FIPS 206 finalises and the Gaussian-sampling subtleties get bored down by the implementation community. The decision-maker is the protocol-design lead. Estimated timeline: three to six months once the wallet hardware vendors have been polled on what they can do in TEEs, which is engineering scope rather than research scope.

## 3. PQ threshold-encryption committee composition

Per §7.0, PQ-Crypsinous's encrypted mempool relies on a per-epoch stake-weighted threshold-encryption committee that decrypts mempool ciphertexts only after committing to ordering. The committee is also the same committee that Chronos pins epoch boundaries against and the same committee that handles Crypsinous flow-encryption per §7.0. The §15 issue #3 calls out that the per-epoch stake-weighted shape is the obvious answer, but the specific PQ threshold scheme is undecided. Closed looks like a named threshold-encryption scheme with a security proof, a Plonky3-friendly key-update protocol for committee rotation, and a reference implementation. The relevant prior art is Penumbra's flow-encryption (curve-based, needs a PQ port) and the [leanMultisig family](https://eprint.iacr.org/2025/055) for hash-based threshold schemes.

The question is open because the published PQ threshold-encryption literature is thinner than the literature on PQ signatures or PQ KEMs. NIST has standardised ML-KEM and the signature schemes named above, but threshold variants of those primitives are still active research. Penumbra's flow-encryption is the cleanest existing design for blockchain mempool encryption, but it is curve-based and the PQ port is non-trivial: the underlying threshold ElGamal becomes either a threshold lattice-KEM or a hash-based commit-reveal scheme, neither of which has a drop-in published replacement. Lattice-based threshold KEMs exist (Boneh et al., several follow-ups) but none have the production-grade scrutiny that ML-KEM has. Hash-based threshold encryption is mostly a commit-reveal pattern rather than a proper threshold encryption, which creates timing assumptions at the consensus layer that complicate Chronos's clock-synchronisation guarantees.

The decision space is two main options plus a hybrid. Option (a) is a lattice-based threshold KEM derived from ML-KEM, with the threshold structure built on top of Shamir secret sharing over the lattice. Cost: needs a security analysis specific to the threshold variant; Module-LWE assumption inherited. Benefit: composes cleanly with the rest of the §1 lattice stack, signatures and KEM share parameter conventions. Option (b) is a hash-based threshold scheme using leanMultisig or similar. Cost: probably commit-reveal rather than true threshold encryption, which means the mempool ordering has to commit before reveal happens, introducing a one-round latency penalty. Benefit: no new cryptographic assumptions beyond hash-function security. The hybrid uses ML-KEM for the encryption layer with a hash-based threshold of decryption shares; Penumbra's design pattern adapts to this with about the same complexity as the pure-lattice option.

This question gates §7.0 PQ-Crypsinous (without a threshold scheme there is no encrypted mempool), §7.1 Chronos (the same committee pins clock boundaries), and §7.3 the combined consensus protocol. The mempool-encryption layer is a hard requirement for the no-MEV property the spec emphasises in §5 and §8. It is genesis-blocking only if the testnet ships with mempool encryption from day one; an alternative is to launch the testnet with plaintext mempool and add encryption in a v1.1 or v1.2 upgrade. The protocol owner has to make that call, but the cleaner option is to pin the committee scheme before genesis so that the committee infrastructure is in place from block 1.

Resolution probably involves engaging a research group with experience in threshold cryptography (Boneh's Stanford group, the Penumbra team, or one of several European academic groups working on lattice threshold schemes). The deliverable is a security-analysis paper plus a reference implementation plus a Plonky3 circuit for the encryption / decryption operations that need to verify on-chain. Six to twelve months, parallel to the §15 issue #1 VRF work because the threshold scheme is similarly research-paper-shaped. The decision-maker is the protocol-design lead in coordination with whichever research group is engaged. The decision is engineering-shaped after the research deliverable lands; before then it is research-paper-shaped.

## 4. Claim-window length

Per §4.4, the Omega protocol enforces a finite claim window followed by deterministic burn-and-reissue at cutoff. Unclaimed pre-fork value is burned and re-minted to the protocol treasury, which funds the §6.3 archival bounty. The §15 issue #4 lists four candidate window lengths: 5, 7, 10, or 20 years. Closed looks like a single number written into the genesis-block protocol parameters with a CIP-1694-shaped governance vote backing it. The empirical anchor is the Mt Gox creditor wait, which ran ten years from the 2014 collapse to the 2024 distribution and was operationally tolerable; creditors did not riot, the legal infrastructure held, and most claimants retrieved their value at the end.

The reason this is open is that the four options each have a clean argument and none of them dominate. Five years is short enough that the pre-fork Ed25519 keys remain ahead of the quantum threat under most published timelines for cryptographically relevant quantum computers. Twenty years is long enough that vault-holders who set up cold-storage in 2025 still have a recovery window through 2046, which matches realistic inheritance and incapacity scenarios. Seven and ten are the middle-ground compromises. The trade-off is genuinely a values question, not a cryptographic one: shorter windows compress the asymmetric-extraction window during which an attacker who recovers pre-fork keys can claim before the legitimate holder, but they punish dormant holders who might have legitimate reasons to wait a decade. The decision is irreducibly governance-shaped.

The decision space is the four numbers above plus their hybrids. A flat five-year window is the most defensive against quantum acceleration but punishes long-term cold storage. A flat twenty-year window is the most forgiving but requires assuming the pre-fork Ed25519 keys remain unbroken for that long, which is a strong claim against the cited NIST PQC operational targets of 2030 to 2035. The hybrid options are tiered: a five-year primary window with a seven-year extension for opted-in claimants who post a non-trivial bond, or a ten-year primary window with mandatory re-attestation at year five (proving the holder is still alive and in possession of the keys, without revealing which keys). Tiered options preserve some of the defensive properties of the short window without writing off the long-tail dormant holders. No major chain has shipped a comparable mechanic; Mt Gox's ten-year wait was a legal-process timeline rather than a protocol parameter.

This question gates §4.4, §6.3 archival bounty (which is funded by the burn-and-reissue), and the genesis-block protocol parameters. It is genesis-blocking because the parameter has to be in the genesis-block at deploy time. It is not v1.0 blocking on the construction side; the T1 commitment work does not depend on which window is chosen. The downstream effects on user perception are large: a five-year window will be read as aggressive by long-term holders, a twenty-year window will be read as careless by quantum-anxious cryptographers. The protocol owner has to be ready to defend the choice publicly.

Resolution is a CIP-1694-shaped governance vote, with the protocol-design lead proposing a default value and the broader Cardano DRep / SPO / Constitutional Committee process making the binding decision. The realistic timeline is one to three governance cycles, which is roughly six to nine months in CIP-1694's published cadence. The protocol owner can ship the testnet with a placeholder value and finalise via vote before mainnet genesis. The decision-maker is the governance system itself, not any single party. My personal lean: ten years primary, with a seven-year cutoff for any address that has not signed any Cardano-side activity since the snapshot block. The Mt Gox precedent gives confidence that ten is operationally tolerable; the seven-year cutoff for known-dormant addresses compresses the asymmetric-extraction window for the highest-risk subset.

## 5. Guardrails-script entrenchment depth

Per §13.1, the Layer 1 guardrails script statically rejects any parameter-update proposal that would replace the genesis commitment, introduce a master / recovery / escrow key, or remove the PLUME nullifier requirement from the verifier circuit. The §15 issue #5 calls out an unresolved design choice: whether the guardrails script itself is forbidden from being updated entirely, or whether it can be updated by a higher-than-supermajority quorum (90% DRep + 90% SPO + unanimous Constitutional Committee, for example). The §13.1 spec text leans toward "forbidden entirely; only chain replacement can change it," but the §15 issue acknowledges the higher-quorum option preserves a theoretical escape hatch. Closed looks like a CIP-1694-shaped binding decision encoded into the guardrails script's own update logic.

The question is open because the two options have genuinely different failure modes. Forbidden-entirely is the clean version: the guardrails script is part of the protocol's identity, and updating it produces a different protocol, full stop. The honest tension §13.1 already names is that "different protocol" still means "different chain," and the wallet ecosystem decides which chain is Omega via reproducible-build attestations. The higher-quorum option preserves the theoretical possibility of fixing a discovered bug in the guardrails script itself without forcing a chain replacement, but the existence of a quorum threshold creates an attack target: if the threshold is reachable in principle, captured stake can drive toward it.

The decision space is binary plus a few quorum-tuning variants. Forbidden-entirely (the §13.1 default) means the only path to changing the guardrails script is a chain replacement that the wallet ecosystem opts into via reproducible builds. The Steem-to-Hive precedent is the operational template. The cost is that any future legitimate need to update the guardrails (a new class of backdoor-shaped parameter that the original drafters did not foresee) routes through chain replacement, which is high friction. Higher-quorum (the alternative) sets the threshold at, say, 90% DRep approval plus 90% SPO approval plus unanimous Constitutional Committee approval. The cost is that the threshold creates a target; the benefit is that legitimate updates have a path that does not require the wallet ecosystem to coordinate a fork. Tezos has shipped supermajority governance for protocol upgrades since 2018 with good operational results, but Tezos does not have an equivalent of the guardrails script.

This question gates §13.1 directly, the §15 issue #5 itself, and the social-fork pre-commitment language in §13.1 Layer 3 because the wallet ecosystem's commitment depends on what counts as "Omega" in the first place. It is genesis-blocking because the guardrails script's update logic is itself encoded in the script. It is not v1.0-blocking on the commitment construction side. The downstream effect on the spec is small (one section), but the downstream effect on the protocol's identity is large: the choice frames whether Omega's security model rests on social coordination or on chain mechanics.

Resolution is a CIP-1694-shaped governance vote with the protocol-design lead proposing a default. The realistic default, given the no-backdoor stance the spec emphasises throughout, is forbidden-entirely. The chain-replacement escape hatch is good enough; legitimate updates are rare; the cleaner property is worth the friction cost. The decision-maker is the governance system. Timeline: one governance cycle, three to six months. My personal lean: forbidden entirely. The higher-quorum option saves friction in scenarios that almost never arise, at the cost of creating a target that exists from genesis onward.

## 6. Plutus → Starstream translation

Per §14.1, claim_script transactions accept any Starstream coroutine that produces a script-hash semantically equivalent to the original Plutus script, but the spec punts the translation work to "a community / compiler-team responsibility." The §15 issue #6 names three sub-decisions inside this. First, which Plutus Core semantics is the source-of-truth: the [RuntimeVerification K-spec](https://github.com/runtimeverification/plutus-core-semantics) or the Agda formal-ledger spec? Second, is translation automated by a compiler, or holder-submitted on a per-script basis? Third, is script-hash equivalence verified by the protocol (mechanically, in the verifier circuit), or attested via dApp re-deployment (socially, by the application's maintainers)? Closed looks like a written protocol-level commitment to one answer for each sub-decision plus a reference implementation of whichever path is chosen.

The question is open because Plutus → Starstream translation is genuinely hard. The two source-of-truth options have different strengths: the K-spec is executable and has been used to find bugs in Plutus Core implementations, but it has not been fully maintained in step with recent Plutus updates. The Agda spec is more current and is the spec the Cardano Foundation cites in formal-verification work, but Agda is harder to extract to running code than K is. Automated translation is the cleaner promise but historically hard for any non-trivial source language; the Move-to-Sui-Move translator and the Solidity-to-zkSync-Solidity work are partial precedents that show the engineering scope. Holder-submitted translation is operationally simpler but raises the question of who arbitrates equivalence disputes.

The decision space is roughly six combinations of three binary choices. Source-of-truth: K-spec or Agda. Translation mode: automated compiler or holder-submitted. Equivalence verification: in-protocol or dApp-redeployment-attested. The cleanest combination is K-spec source-of-truth + automated compiler + in-protocol verification, which yields a fully mechanical pipeline where any Plutus script translates to an equivalent Starstream coroutine and the protocol verifies equivalence in-circuit. The cost is that this is a multi-year compiler engineering effort plus a Plonky3 circuit that verifies Plutus-Core equivalence inside a STARK. The realistic combination is K-spec source-of-truth + holder-submitted + dApp-redeployment-attested, which is what the §14.1 punt language suggests: the holder submits a Starstream coroutine, the dApp's maintainers attest to equivalence, and the protocol surface accepts any submission that the dApp signs off on. The cost there is that the protocol's trust assumption now includes the dApp maintainer.

This question gates §4.3 claim_script policy and §14.1's "submit a Starstream module that the verifier accepts" language. It does not gate genesis or T1 commitment construction. It does not gate T2 consensus or T6 verifier. The ungatedness is the saving grace: the protocol can ship without a Plutus-translation answer, and claim_script can default to the conservative "banned without dual signature from registered deployer key" path from §4.3 until the translation pipeline matures. The friction cost is that dApp migration to Omega lags the rest of the protocol's launch, which is a UX hit but not a security or correctness hit.

Resolution is upstream-tracking-shaped: the work happens in the compiler community (the RuntimeVerification team, the IOG Plutus team, the LFDT-Nightstream Starstream team, plus whoever else picks it up), and Omega's protocol surface adapts to whatever the compiler community ships. The Omega protocol's responsibility is to keep claim_script's policy clean and unambiguous (banned without re-attestation) until the upstream translation matures. Timeline is years, not months: realistic delivery is one to three years for a production-grade Plutus → Starstream compiler. The decision-maker is the upstream community plus the dApp ecosystem; Omega protocol governance only steps in to update the §4.3 policy once the upstream deliverable lands. My personal lean: punt, document, do not block.

## 7. Starstream upstream maturity tracking

Per §14.3, Starstream is in active design with implementation experiments. The upstream [`impl-plan.md`](https://github.com/LFDT-Nightstream/Starstream/blob/main/impl-plan.md) lists the compiler, interpreter, and WebAssembly target as shipping, with the type checker, IVC (incrementally verifiable computation), MCC (memory consistency check), and lookups modules marked TODO. The §15 issue #7 calls out that Omega's T3 (smart-contract VM) and T6 (verifier circuit) tracks depend on these landing upstream rather than re-implementing them from scratch. Closed looks like upstream tags or releases for each TODO module, plus an integration plan that pins specific Starstream commits to specific Omega versions.

The reason this is upstream-tracking-shaped rather than research-paper-shaped or engineering-shaped on the Omega side is that the Starstream team owns the work. They have published the implementation plan, they have shipped substantial parts of it, and the remaining modules are scheduled. Omega's role is to consume what they ship, not to drive the schedule. This contrasts with the §15 issue #1 VRF question, where Omega has to actively commission or pin a paper because no one else owns the deliverable. Starstream's TODO list is on someone else's roadmap, and that roadmap is public.

The decision space is mostly about coupling, not about scope. Option (a) is to fork Starstream and add the TODO modules in-tree, treating Omega's T3 / T6 as the primary integrators. Cost: maintenance burden, divergence from upstream, double engineering effort. Benefit: schedule control. Option (b) is to track upstream releases, contribute back any Omega-specific extensions, and pin specific commits. Cost: schedule dependency on upstream cadence. Benefit: shared engineering effort, no divergence, the LFDT governance umbrella applies. Option (c) is a hybrid: track upstream but maintain a small Omega-specific patch set for any Crypsinous-shielding hooks that don't fit upstream's scope. The §14.4 language suggests option (c) is the working assumption: Starstream is upstream, Omega-specific shielding hooks are local. Other chains that have tracked upstream zkVM projects (Aleo's tracking of the snarkVM ecosystem, several rollups' tracking of Plonky2 → Plonky3) suggest option (c) is operationally sustainable.

This question gates T3 (smart-contract VM) and T6 (verifier circuit) timelines, but it does not gate T1 commitment work or T2 consensus or genesis. The Starstream maturity question is post-fork: the post-claim execution layer cannot ship until the upstream modules ship. If the upstream schedule slips, T3 and T6 slip with it, but the rest of the program continues. The downstream effect is on user-facing claim flows: claim_utxo can ship with a minimal Starstream output (a Starstream UTxO with no coroutine logic) before the type checker and IVC land; the more sophisticated claim_script and atomic-bundle flows need the full Starstream stack.

Resolution is upstream-tracking by definition. Concrete next steps: pin a specific Starstream commit as the "Omega T3 reference target" each time an Omega release ships; contribute back any patches Omega makes during integration; participate in LFDT-Nightstream governance as a downstream consumer. Timeline is dictated by upstream's `impl-plan.md`, which the Starstream team has published. The decision-maker for Omega-side adaptation is the protocol-engineering lead in coordination with the LFDT-Nightstream community. No CIP-1694 vote needed; this is engineering-shaped tracking with a clear upstream owner. My lean: track, contribute back where Omega can, do not fork.

## 8. Filecoin PQ-port scope

Per §6.5, the mirror partnerchain is a fork of [Filecoin](https://github.com/filecoin-project) under the [Cardano partnerchains SDK](https://github.com/input-output-hk/partner-chains), with all curve cryptography (ECDSA, BLS, Groth16) replaced by the §1 PQ stack. The §15 issue #8 estimates the engineering scope at six to twelve months, comparable to Filecoin's original mainnet launch from spec, but acknowledges the actual work breakdown is unscoped. Three sub-decisions inside this question. First, which Filecoin actor types port first (the storage market actor, the miner actor, the payment-channel actor)? Second, is the fork at the protocol-spec level or at the lotus-implementation level? Third, what is the test-network bootstrap path? Closed looks like a written engineering plan with milestone deliverables, an actor-by-actor port schedule, and a test-network launch date.

The question is open because the engineering scope is genuinely large and the choices interact. Filecoin has roughly a dozen actor types in its built-in actors crate, each with its own state machine and signature surface. Some (the storage market and miner actors) are the load-bearing ones; others (the payment-channel actor, the multisig actor) are less critical to the mirror's role as a §6.5 retrieval layer. The protocol-spec versus lotus-implementation question is a philosophical one: Filecoin maintains both a spec and a primary Go implementation (lotus), plus a Rust implementation (forest). Forking at the spec level means writing a new implementation from the spec; forking at the lotus level means modifying lotus directly. Spec-level fork is cleaner but is more engineering work; lotus-level fork is faster but inherits lotus's bugs and design choices.

The decision space is roughly four options. Option (a) is a spec-level fork with a from-scratch Rust implementation built on top of the Cardano partnerchain SDK's Rust ecosystem. Cost: highest engineering effort, probably twelve to eighteen months. Benefit: cleanest separation, no upstream maintenance burden. Option (b) is a lotus-level fork with PQ replacements applied incrementally to lotus. Cost: inherits lotus's design; ongoing upstream sync. Benefit: fastest to working test-network, probably six to nine months. Option (c) is a forest-level fork (the Rust Filecoin implementation), which gets the Rust ecosystem benefits without writing a new implementation from scratch. Cost: inherits forest's maturity gaps relative to lotus. Benefit: reasonable middle ground. Option (d) is a hybrid: lotus-level fork for the test-network plus a spec-level Rust implementation for mainnet. This is what some other chains have done when porting Ethereum infrastructure.

This question gates §6.5 mirror partnerchain, the §7.2 Minotaur PoSpaceTime resource (which depends on the mirror chain existing), and the §13 archival bounty mechanics (which depend on the storage providers having a chain to operate on). It does not gate genesis or T1 commitment work; the mirror partnerchain can ship months or years after genesis without affecting the core claim flow. The downstream effect on Omega's consensus security model is large: Minotaur's multi-resource property depends on the storage resource being meaningful, which depends on the mirror partnerchain having real storage providers with bonded capacity. Without the mirror, Minotaur degrades to single-resource PoS, which the spec specifically wants to avoid per §7.2.

Resolution is engineering-shaped: a written engineering plan, an actor-by-actor port schedule, and a milestone-driven implementation. The realistic timeline for the full port is six to twelve months for a basic test-network plus another six to twelve months for mainnet readiness. The Filecoin original mainnet launch took roughly two years from announcement to mainnet; a port should be faster because the protocol is already specified. The decision-maker is the protocol-engineering lead in coordination with whichever team is contracted for the Filecoin port (could be IOG, could be a third-party team with Filecoin experience). My lean: option (c), a forest-level fork, because the Rust ecosystem benefits compound with the rest of the Omega stack, and forest is mature enough by 2026 to be a reasonable starting point.

## 9. Minotaur weighting parameter ω

Per §7.2, Minotaur's security inequality is `ω · β_w + (1−ω) · β_s < 1/2`, where `β_w` is the adversarial fraction of the storage resource and `β_s` is the adversarial fraction of the stake resource, and `ω ∈ [0, 1]` is the weighting. The §15 issue #9 names three sub-questions: the initial value of `ω`, the rotation policy (how often and under what process can `ω` change), and the constitutional constraints (per §13.1 guardrails, can governance push `ω = 1` and effectively disable storage-resource consensus?). Closed looks like an initial value baked into the genesis-block parameters, a rotation cadence with a CIP-1694-shaped vote process, and a clear answer on whether the §13.1 guardrails treat `ω = 1` as a backdoor-shaped parameter update.

The question is open because `ω` is simultaneously a tuning knob (which the protocol designer can pick reasonable defaults for) and a potential attack surface (an attacker who captures stake governance can push `ω` toward 1 to make the captured-stake majority decisive). The §13.1 guardrails script statically rejects parameter updates that "introduce a master / recovery / escrow key field" and similar backdoor-shaped changes, but `ω` is not a key. It is a weighting. The line between "tuning knob" and "backdoor-shaped" is not obvious for `ω` in a way it is obvious for, say, an "introduce a regulator-master key" parameter update. The §13.1 spec text does not directly address `ω`, which is what the §15 issue is calling out.

The decision space splits into three sub-decisions. For initial value, the candidates are `ω = 0.5` (equal weighting), `ω = 0.3` (storage-favoured, which gives the storage resource the larger share of consensus weight in the inequality), `ω = 0.7` (stake-favoured, which gives stake the larger share). The Minotaur paper does not pick a value; it proves the inequality holds for any `ω`. Equal weighting is the cleanest default; storage-favoured leans into the §6.5 mirror partnerchain coupling; stake-favoured matches Cardano's existing economic model. For rotation policy, the candidates are: never rotate (`ω` is locked at genesis), rotate via a standard CIP-1694 vote with no special quorum, rotate only via a high-quorum vote subject to the same guardrails as §13.1 Layer 1. For constitutional constraint, the candidates are: `ω` can move freely in `[0, 1]`, `ω` is bounded to `[0.1, 0.9]` to prevent disabling either resource, `ω` updates require dual-resource majorities (both stake-side and storage-side approval) to prevent single-resource governance capture from altering the weighting.

This question gates §7.2 Minotaur PQ, §13.1 guardrails script (which has to decide whether `ω` is a guardrails-relevant parameter), and the §6.5 mirror partnerchain economic model (which depends on storage having meaningful consensus weight). It is genesis-blocking on the initial value: the protocol cannot ship without picking an `ω`. It is post-genesis on the rotation policy and constitutional constraint, which can be set by the early CIP-1694 process. The downstream effect on consensus is large: a poorly-chosen initial `ω` can make the chain effectively single-resource, which gives up the §7.2 security argument.

Resolution is partly research-paper-shaped (does the Minotaur security inequality have a sweet spot for a chain with Omega's properties?) and partly governance-shaped (the rotation policy and constitutional constraint are CIP-1694 questions). For the initial value, the realistic default is `ω = 0.5` plus a constraint that `ω` cannot leave `[0.1, 0.9]` post-genesis without a dual-resource majority. This treats `ω` as a tuning knob inside the bounds and as a guardrails-relevant parameter outside the bounds, which is the cleanest line. The decision-maker for the initial value is the protocol-design lead, informed by Minotaur authors' guidance if available. The decision-maker for the rotation policy is the governance system. Timeline: six months for the initial value (research plus design plus genesis-parameter freeze), one to three governance cycles after genesis for the rotation policy. My lean: `ω = 0.5` initial, dual-resource-majority required for any move outside `[0.1, 0.9]`, treated as guardrails-relevant for the out-of-bounds case but not for the in-bounds case.

## 10. Mirror partnerchain economic model

Per §6.5, the mirror partnerchain is a forked Filecoin running under the Cardano partnerchains SDK, with storage providers earning revenue from two streams: per-retrieval fees from holders (paid in mirror-chain native asset, redeemable for ωADA via the partnerchain bridge) and Omega-side block rewards via the partnerchain coupling. The §15 issue #10 calls out that the split between the two revenue streams, the per-retrieval price floor, and the long-term sustainability under declining ωADA prices are all unscoped. Closed looks like a written economic spec with a fee structure, a price-floor mechanism, and a sensitivity analysis under various ωADA price scenarios over a multi-decade horizon.

The question is open because mirror-chain economics is a multi-decade design problem and the relevant precedents are thin. Filecoin's existing economic model has worked operationally since 2020, but Filecoin is a single-asset chain whose token price drives storage-provider profitability directly. The mirror partnerchain has two assets (its own native asset and ωADA), two revenue streams, and a long-tail customer base (holders claiming in 2046 are the relevant retrievers, not high-volume retrievers in 2026). Sustainability under declining token prices is a known failure mode for storage chains: if the token price drops below the operating cost of running storage hardware, providers exit, and the chain's storage capacity collapses. Filecoin has had several such scares since launch.

The decision space is mostly about how the two revenue streams interact. Option (a) is to make per-retrieval fees the dominant stream, with Omega-side block rewards as a small bonus. Cost: low retrieval volumes in early years mean storage providers are unprofitable until claim activity ramps. Benefit: fee-driven economics scale naturally with usage. Option (b) is to make Omega-side block rewards the dominant stream, funded out of the protocol treasury. Cost: treasury depletion if usage stays low; storage providers are insulated from market signals. Benefit: predictable provider income. Option (c) is a balanced split (50/50 by long-run revenue) with a price-floor mechanism: if per-retrieval fees fall below a floor, the treasury makes up the difference; if they exceed the floor, the treasury contribution decreases. The price-floor mechanism is the interesting design lever; it matches what some commodity markets use to stabilise producer income. Other storage chains (Arweave's endowment, Storj's pre-paid contracts) have shipped variants of these patterns.

This question gates §6.5 mirror partnerchain economics, §6.3 archival bounty (which is a separate but related funding source), and §13 governance (which has to set the per-retrieval price floor and the treasury allocation). It does not gate genesis; the mirror partnerchain can ship with a v1.0 economic model that gets refined over time. The downstream effect on Omega's archive availability in 2046 is large: if the mirror chain's economics fail, the chain stops storing snapshot data, and holders who relied on the mirror lose their cheap retrieval path. The §6.3 archival bounty plus self-storage by holders are the fallbacks, but they are weaker fallbacks than a healthy mirror partnerchain.

Resolution is engineering-shaped on the spec side and governance-shaped on the parameter side. Concrete next steps: write a published economic-model spec for the mirror partnerchain with sensitivity analysis under multiple ωADA price scenarios; pick an initial fee structure and price-floor mechanism via the protocol-design lead; iterate based on testnet data once the mirror chain runs. The decision-maker for the economic-model spec is the protocol-design lead, possibly in consultation with token-economics specialists. The decision-maker for parameter values is the governance system. Timeline: six months for the spec, one to two years of testnet data before mainnet parameter freeze, ongoing governance cadence after mainnet. My lean: balanced split with a treasury-backed price floor, recognising that the price floor will need governance attention every few years and accepting that as a feature rather than a bug.

## Cross-cutting patterns

The ten questions sort into four shapes by what kind of work resolves them. The shapes overlap; any given question has a primary shape and sometimes a secondary one. Knowing the shape tells you what kind of decision-maker to engage and what kind of deliverable to expect.

The research-paper-shaped questions are Q1 (hash-based VRF) and partially Q9 (Minotaur ω initial value, where the security analysis benefits from research input). For these, the path is to commission or pin a paper with a security reduction. The deliverable is a publication plus a reference implementation. The timeline is six to twelve months and the decision-maker is the protocol-design lead in coordination with the research community. These are the highest-stakes questions because they gate consensus, which gates everything else.

The governance-shaped questions are Q4 (claim-window length), Q5 (guardrails-script entrenchment depth), Q9 (Minotaur ω rotation policy and constitutional constraint), and Q10 (mirror partnerchain economic model parameters). For these, the path is a CIP-1694-shaped vote with the protocol-design lead proposing a default. The deliverable is a binding governance decision encoded into protocol parameters. The timeline is one to three governance cycles, three to nine months in CIP-1694's published cadence. The decision-maker is the governance system itself, not any single party. These are the questions where the protocol owner has to be ready to defend their proposed defaults publicly.

The engineering-shaped questions are Q2 (lattice-vs-hash signature decision), Q3 (PQ threshold-encryption committee composition), Q8 (Filecoin PQ-port scope), and Q10 (mirror partnerchain economic model spec). For these, the path is a written engineering plan with milestone deliverables and a project-management cadence. The deliverable is a working implementation. The timelines vary from three months (signature decision, mostly benchmarking) to twelve to eighteen months (Filecoin port, full implementation). The decision-maker is the protocol-engineering lead in coordination with whichever team owns the implementation work. These are the questions where the answer is conditional on scope and effort, and where the right move is to commit to a milestone schedule.

The upstream-tracking-shaped questions are Q6 (Plutus → Starstream translation) and Q7 (Starstream upstream maturity). For these, the path is to track an external project's roadmap, contribute back where useful, and adapt the Omega protocol surface to whatever the upstream community ships. The deliverable on Omega's side is a clean policy that defaults conservatively until the upstream matures. The timeline is dictated by upstream and ranges from one to several years. The decision-maker for Omega-side adaptation is the protocol-engineering lead, but the underlying work happens elsewhere. These are the questions where Omega's job is to not block: ship the rest of the protocol, default conservatively on the affected surface, integrate when the upstream lands.

What ties the four shapes together is a single property: none of these ten questions are unscoped or unanswerable. Each has a clear path to closure, a clear decision-maker, and a defensible default the protocol-design lead can propose. The ten questions are the genuine open issues from §15 of the design spec; everything else has been brainstormed, pressure-tested, and integrated into the spec. The work to close them is real, but it is bounded.
